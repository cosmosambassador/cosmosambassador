# The Tri-Vector Vacuum Landscape

**Author:** Theresa J. Morris  
**Date:** July 4, 2026  
**Status:** Conceptual research white paper; not a claim of discovery

## Abstract

This paper proposes the Tri-Vector Vacuum Landscape, a framework for comparing three observable cosmic histories: expansion, structure growth, and dark-energy behavior. The central idea is to compare observations with the standard model and search for a correlated residual across all three.

Tri-Vector observables:

- H(z): expansion history
- Gstr(z): structure-growth history
- w(z): effective dark-energy state

The hypothesis is not that a new particle has been discovered. It is that a stable correlated residual across these three observables could indicate missing dynamics in the assumed vacuum sector.

## The problem

Quantum field theory, gravitation, and cosmology do not yet provide a complete account of why the observed effective vacuum energy is so small and positive. Three questions must be separated:

1. Why do large vacuum contributions cancel or fail to gravitate as naively expected?
2. Why is the observed effective value not exactly zero?
3. Is the dark-energy sector truly constant, or can it evolve?

The deeper gravitational object is not merely a number called vacuum energy but the renormalized vacuum stress-energy state.

The central question is whether that state is truly constant with cosmic time.

## Casimir, supersymmetry, and the residue

The Casimir effect shows that changing physical conditions can alter allowed field modes and measurable vacuum-related stresses. It does not prove cosmological vacuum evolution. It motivates the narrower question: can changing cosmic conditions modify effective vacuum stress?

Exact supersymmetry can produce cancellation between bosonic and fermionic zero-point contributions. The cosmological puzzle is a tiny nonzero remainder rather than perfect zero.

The research question is: what, if anything, determines that residue?

## Weinberg and the landscape

Weinberg's 1987 anthropic argument did not derive the cosmological constant microscopically. It asked how large positive vacuum energy could be before expansion prevented gravitational collapse into galaxies. Its elegance was to convert an unknown mechanism into a calculable condition.

A complete framework requires:

Fundamental law -> possible states -> dynamics -> selection.

The Tri-Vector framework extends the selection object from one fixed number to an evolutionary trajectory.

## Observable Tri-Vector

The decisive test is whether the three residuals move together:

Delta H <-> Delta Gstr <-> Delta w.

One anomaly may be noise. A stable repeated cross-survey correlation may indicate missing physics.

## Research rule

**Do not invent the particle first. Find the correlated residual first.**

Correlated anomaly -> field model -> quantization -> prediction -> new observation.

## Conclusion

The Tri-Vector Vacuum Landscape asks whether dark energy is a fixed property of space or one observable aspect of a deeper evolving vacuum structure. It proposes no confirmed new quantum. It proposes a measurement architecture in which expansion, structure growth, and dark-energy behavior are tested together.
