# The Elephant Principle of Frontier Physics

**Author:** Theresa J. Morris  
**Date:** July 4, 2026  
**Status:** Philosophy-of-science companion paper

## Thesis

The observable universe may be compared to an elephant examined by observers who can touch only one part at a time.

Plasma physics measures collective charged matter and electromagnetic fields. Particle physics measures excitations and interactions of quantum fields. Cosmology measures expansion, structure, and large-scale geometry. Quantum computing manipulates superposition, interference, entanglement, and measurement. Dark-matter research measures unexplained gravitational behavior. Dark-energy research measures unexplained cosmic acceleration.

Each field may be observing a real part of nature. None should automatically be assumed to observe the whole.

Observation is not the whole of reality.

## The frontier problem

The central frontier problem may not be only lack of data. It may also be the fragmentation of observation into disciplines that each describe one projection of a deeper physical system.

A candidate whole may include interacting contributions from:

- quantum behavior
- plasma and electromagnetic collectivity
- gravitation
- vacuum state
- information
- spacetime structure

The unknown relation among them is the whole elephant.

## Method

The Tri-Vector approach searches for:

Multiple projections -> shared residual -> underlying relation.

The governing principle is:

**Observation is a window into reality, not the whole of reality.**

The elephant is not the trunk. The universe is not any single instrument, field, particle, or equation used to measure it.
